# Paper 4: The Path (Siraat)

## E156 Body (153 words, 7 sentences)

What would it take to close the evidence gap in the world's worst research deserts? Using coefficients from the hurdle model and Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition, we computed counterfactual trial densities for the 30 largest evidence deserts under scenarios of improved governance and health expenditure. The Blinder-Oaxaca decomposition showed that 61.7% of the HIC-LMIC trial gap is explained by differences in endowments (GDP, health spend, governance), while 15% reflects structural inequity that persists even at equivalent covariate levels. If governance quality in the 30 largest deserts reached 0.38 — the GAM-identified threshold — and health expenditure rose to 5% of GDP, the model predicts a 3.2-fold increase in trial activity, potentially bringing 22 of 30 countries above the zero-trial threshold. Quantile regression confirmed that governance matters most at the bottom of the distribution (Q25 coefficient=+0.72 versus Q75=+0.27), indicating that reform disproportionately benefits the worst-served countries. GDP per capita remains the binding constraint for the poorest nations, where even optimal governance would be insufficient without direct research investment. Counterfactual estimates depend on the assumption that cross-sectional associations reflect achievable transitions.
