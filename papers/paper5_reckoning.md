# Paper 5: The Reckoning (Hisab)

## E156 Body (156 words, 7 sentences)

Is the global distribution of clinical research becoming more or less equitable over time? We computed five inequality measures annually from 2005 to 2023 across 180 countries: Gini coefficient, concentration index ranked by GDP, Theil index with between/within decomposition, Kakwani progressivity index, and KL divergence from burden-proportional allocation. The Gini coefficient was 0.915 in 2005 and 0.893 in 2023 (bootstrap 95% CI: 0.823-0.929), representing extreme inequality with negligible improvement over nearly two decades. The concentration index of 0.60 confirmed heavy pro-rich concentration, while the Kakwani index of -0.05 demonstrated regressive allocation — research systematically avoids higher-burden countries. The Theil decomposition revealed that 86% of inequality is within income groups rather than between them, meaning the research divide is not simply rich-versus-poor but reflects structural exclusion even among similar economies. KL divergence from a burden-proportional "fair" distribution was 1.11 bits, indicating extreme departure from equitable allocation. Two decades of global health initiatives have failed to meaningfully redistribute where healing knowledge is produced. This analysis captures only ClinicalTrials.gov-registered studies and does not account for observational or implementation research.
